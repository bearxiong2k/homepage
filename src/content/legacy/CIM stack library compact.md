# Library of close investigation on related works

## Ying Wang’s ICT-CAS memory-centric accelerator group

### CIM-MLC

- CIM-MLC is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework with a configurable CIM hardware abstraction and instruction-like meta-operator output. Its real contribution is the three-level CIM abstraction—Core Mode, Crossbar Mode, and Wordline Mode—and a corresponding multi-level scheduling pipeline that lowers static ONNX DNN graphs into meta-operator flows for several CIM accelerator styles. The paper’s strongest evidence is for DNN inference mapping/scheduling across selected CIM architecture templates; its weakest evidence is for claims of universality, backend replaceability, IR auditability, cycle fidelity, and reproducibility. I would classify it primarily as a compiler/mapping framework, secondarily as a narrow/hidden-stack end-to-end flow and a hardware-software co-design abstraction demo. The paper claims a “universal multi-level compilation framework” and open-source release, but the artifact is explicitly described as a beta version and is tightly coupled to ONNX, Python configs, mutated graph attributes, and hard-coded scheduling/codegen assumptions.

### Hermes

- Hermes / NDP-DIMM is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo with a narrow end-to-end LLM inference flow, plus a modeling/simulation backend for a specific digital near-data-processing DIMM architecture. Its real contribution to a CIM infrastructure stack is a specialized mapping/scheduling strategy for activation-sparse transformer inference across one consumer GPU and multiple NDP-DIMMs, evaluated with an in-house Ramulator-derived simulator, measured GPU kernels, and RTL-synthesized NDP cores. The strongest stack layers are the hot/cold neuron placement abstraction, online predictor, and window-based DIMM load-balancing scheduler; the weakest layers for our project are frontend generality, explicit IR, auditability, backend replaceability, and reproducible artifacts.

### Papi

- PAPI is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo and modeling/simulation framework for HBM/DRAM-style PIM-enabled LLM decoding, with a layer-specific contribution in runtime scheduling. The real contribution is a heterogeneous system model with GPU-like processing units, FC-PIM for fully connected kernels, Attn-PIM for attention/KV-cache-heavy work, and a lightweight runtime rule that uses current request-level and token-level parallelism to decide whether FC kernels should run on PUs or FC-PIM. The paper’s strongest evidence is in the architecture, mapping, scheduling heuristic, and simulator-based evaluation; its weakest infrastructure evidence is frontend ingestion, explicit IR, ISA, modular compiler interfaces, artifact reproducibility, and backend replaceability. PAPI is therefore a narrow end-to-end simulated stack and strong isolated scheduler/architecture layer.

### Ouroboros

- Ouroboros is best understood as a narrow end-to-end hardware-software co-design demo plus modeling/simulation framework for one specific target: wafer-scale digital SRAM CIM for LLM inference. Its real stack contribution is a vertically integrated architecture proposal combining token-grained pipelined execution, communication/fault-aware layer-to-core mapping, distributed dynamic KV-cache management, and a custom E2E simulator. The paper’s “end-to-end” claim is evidenced by Fig. 3’s flow from LLM architecture + wafer architecture through pipeline partitioning, MIQP/DP mapping, KV mapping, inter-sequence scheduling, and distributed control, but the interfaces are paper-internal.

### CMSwitch: Be CIM or Be Memory: A Dual-mode-aware DNN Compiler for CIM Accelerators

- CMSwitch is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework for dual-mode CIM accelerators, not as a full community stack. Its real contribution is the formal inclusion of array-level compute/memory mode selection into CIM compilation: a hardware abstraction extension called DEHA, a DACO optimization pass using dynamic programming for network segmentation and MIP/Gurobi for per-segment compute/memory resource allocation, and a small dual-mode meta-operator extension with CM.switch(TOM|TOC, arrayaddr) for code-generation output. The paper’s strongest layer is therefore mapping/scheduling for switchable CIM arrays, with a partial low-level abstraction through meta-operators; its frontend, backend simulator, ISA, reproducibility artifact, and benchmark harness are much weaker or only implicitly specified. The work should be categorized primarily as a compiler/mapping framework and secondarily as a narrow end-to-end stack for ONNX DNN inference on a Dynaplasia-like dual-mode CIM target. The paper itself describes the compiler as taking ONNX DNNs and hardware parameters, producing segments with dual-mode allocation and meta-operators, and reporting a 1.31× average speedup over CIM-MLC, but the reusable stack boundaries and artifact evidence are limited.

## TU Dresden’s Chair for Compiler Construction, headed by Jeronimo Castrillon

### CINM (Cinnamon)

- CINM/Cinnamon’s real contribution is an MLIR-based compiler and abstraction stack for CIM/CNM systems, with explicit hierarchical dialects (cinm, cim, cnm, plus device dialects such as UPMEM and memristor) and lowering passes from higher MLIR dialects toward device APIs or LLVM, rather than a universal, fully validated CIM infrastructure. The paper is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework and narrow end-to-end stack with strong IR inspiration: it demonstrates a real UPMEM CNM backend and a memristor-crossbar CIM backend through gem5/OCC-style simulation, but the paper itself limits evaluation to those targets and says the restriction is due to lack of open-source tools for other architectures.

### C4CAM

- C4CAM is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework with a narrow end-to-end CAM simulation flow. Its real contribution is an MLIR-based lowering path from selected TorchScript comparison/search kernels into a CAM-specific dialect, plus mapping and heuristic optimization passes for a hierarchical CAM accelerator model backed by CAMASim/Eva-CAM-style performance estimates. It is strongest as a CAM-specific IR and mapping abstraction for similarity/search workloads—KNN, HDC similarity, and DNA read mapping—not as a broad frontend, stable ISA, reusable benchmark harness, or hardware-validated full-stack platform. The paper explicitly claims an “end-to-end automated retargetable framework” from TorchScript plus architecture specification to generated code and CAM design-space exploration, but the evidence is mainly MLIR snippets, pass descriptions, simulator integration, and experiments; I found no public C4CAM compiler artifact, only the underlying CAMASim simulator is public.

### Sherlock

- SHERLOCK is best understood as a compiler/mapping/scheduling framework for logic-based NVM compute-in-memory. Its real contribution is a DAG-based operand clustering, column mapping, instruction-generation, and reliability-aware multi-row-activation scheduling method for bulk bitwise operations on memristive NVM arrays, evaluated through an extended gem5 + NVSim + SPICE-modeled backend. The paper claims an end-to-end automated, target-agnostic flow, but the evidenced flow is narrow: C/pycparser bulk-bitwise kernels are lowered to a DAG, mapped to NVM array columns, and emitted as simulator-compatible read/write/shift/CIM instructions for scouting-logic-style ReRAM/STT-MRAM systems.

### CoMoNM

- CoMoNM is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework and strong isolated cost-model layer for compute-near-memory (CNM) systems. Its real contribution is a latency-estimation framework that accepts an application representation, a target CNM system specification, and an explicit hierarchical mapping, then estimates execution time using a virtual assembly layer, a custom CNM IR, and compute/memory execution engines. The strongest infrastructure ideas are the hierarchical mapping specification, the separation of compute-engine and memory-engine modeling, and the attempt to bridge MLIR linalg / affine-style frontends to a target-agnostic virtual CNM assembly called llvcnm. The paper is mainly a latency cost-model backend with partial frontend/IR support, evaluated on UPMEM hardware and Samsung HBM-PIM simulation.

### LearnCNM2Predict

- LearnCNM2Predict: Transfer Learning-based Performance Model for CNM Systems is best understood as a layer-specific performance-modeling / cost-model contribution for UPMEM-style compute-near-memory. Its real stack contribution is a learned transfer-learning predictor that consumes static binary-derived opcode histograms plus UPMEM hardware/compiler/workload parameters and predicts runtime and speedup for UPMEM benchmark variants; the paper validates this on measured UPMEM executions and reports average MAPE around 8.04% for runtime and 7.11% for speedup, with worst cases near 18% for GEMV. The paper’s “application-agnostic” and “compiler-agnostic” language should be interpreted narrowly: the evidence is UPMEM C/C++ benchmark code, UPMEM binaries, compiler pass-sequence variation, DPU/tasklet variation, and cross-benchmark transfer. The closest classification is modeling/performance prediction framework and strong isolated layer, with a useful reproducibility artifact.

### Declarative Memory Services

- Declarative Memory Services is best understood as a taxonomy / vision paper and flexible abstraction proposal for heterogeneous memory services. Its real contribution is a conceptual split between declarative application annotations, a calibration/device-catalog layer, and a memory-services runtime that maps high-level properties such as coherence, persistence, cacheability, bandwidth, latency, offloadability, compression, and replication onto heterogeneous memory devices, including CXL memory and PIM-style computational memory. The evidence is mainly a layered architecture, property taxonomy, device-catalog sketch, and three motivating DBMS-oriented case studies: disaggregated B+-trees, PIM filtering, and tiered-memory buffer caching. It is most accurately classified as Other: declarative memory-services vision.

### Count2Multiply

- Using the final public HPCA 2026 PDF as the target paper, Count2Multiply is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo plus layer-specific CIM arithmetic / fault-tolerance contribution. Its real stack contribution is a digital-CIM masked accumulation primitive built from high-radix Johnson counters, Ambit-style bulk-bitwise DRAM operations, host-generated µPrograms, and an ECC-based recomputation scheme; the strongest parts are the low-level mapping from masked integer accumulation to row operations, the value-dependent command generation, and the cycle-level DRAM evaluation. The authors claim a “digital CIM framework” and compatibility with functionally complete CIM proposals, but the evidenced implementation and evaluation are tightly centered on commodity-DRAM/Ambit-style operations and manually selected GEMV/GEMM-like workloads.

### Efficient In-Memory Acceleration of Sparse Block Diagonal LLMs

- This is best understood as a narrow hardware-software co-design / compiler-mapping framework for one structured-sparsity family: Monarch-style block-diagonal transformer weight matrices mapped onto analog CIM crossbars. Its real contribution is a mapping and scheduling method that packs Monarch block-diagonal matrices into CIM arrays, handles rotations/shifts and folded permutations, then evaluates latency/energy/memory footprint in an existing AIMC simulator. The strongest stack layer is mapping plus mapping-aware scheduling for stationary parameterized matmuls in transformer inference. The “end-to-end automation” claim is partially evidenced by the D2S → mapper → scheduler → simulator flow in Fig. 2, but the frontend contract, IR, pass interfaces, simulator adapter, figure scripts, exact model artifacts, and reproducibility harness are not specified or released.

## Xiaoming Chen / Yinhe Han’s group at ICT-CAS/UCAS

### PIM EDA

- “PIM EDA Suite / PIM Toolchain” is best understood as a narrow end-to-end stack plus several strong layer-specific components for crossbar-based PIM/CIM DNN/CNN inference accelerators. The umbrella repository describes four tools: PIMSYN-NN for architecture synthesis, PIMCOMP-NN for ONNX-to-instruction compilation and mapping/scheduling, PIMSIM-NN for ISA-based behavior/cycle simulation, and PIMACC for accuracy simulation under nonidealities. Its real contribution is a coherent research toolchain around a shared crossbar PIM abstraction, array/macro/core hierarchy, weight replication, dataflow scheduling, pseudo-instructions, and simulator inputs; its limitation is that the “full stack” is narrow, mostly static DNN/CNN inference, ONNX-centered, crossbar/PIM-array-centered, and only partly auditable as an independently specified IR stack.

### PIMSIM-NN

- PIMSIM-NN is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework with a strong ISA-level backend interface, plus a narrow associated compiler flow for ONNX DNN inference. Its real contribution is an instruction-sequence boundary between a compiler and a SystemC-based PIM simulator: the paper claims an ISA, compiler, and cycle-accurate configurable simulator, while the public simulator artifact mainly accepts compressed per-core instruction streams plus architecture/NoC configuration files and points users to PIMCOMP-NN for ONNX-to-instruction compilation. The paper is therefore a layer-specific strong contribution for ISA/simulator decoupling and a narrow end-to-end stack for CNN-style DNN inference on crossbar-based PIM accelerators, not a general frontend-, backend-, or workload-agnostic CIM infrastructure. The DATE’24 late-breaking paper is only two pages, so many details that would matter for auditability, calibration, legality checking, and reproducible benchmarking are either delegated to the ISA/PIMCOMP artifacts or left Unknown / not specified.

### PIMSYN-NN

- PIMSYN-NN is best understood as a compiler/mapping-oriented architecture synthesis and DSE framework for static CNN inference on a parameterized crossbar-based PIM accelerator template. Its strongest contribution is the synthesis flow that jointly searches weight duplication, dataflow DAG generation, macro partitioning, macro sharing, and peripheral component allocation under a power budget; the paper does provide a concrete IR-like DAG abstraction, but that IR is mainly an internal scheduling/cost-model vehicle rather than an auditable, serialized, backend-agnostic community IR. The paper claims “full-stack” and “one-click” conversion from CNN applications to PIM architectures, but the evidenced stack is narrow: ONNX-format CNN structure in, synthesized macro/PE/crossbar architecture and dataflow scheduling out, evaluated by an IR-based behavior-level simulator. The paper is therefore a narrow end-to-end stack and compiler/mapping framework, with useful IR and DSE ideas but limited frontend breadth, limited backend interchangeability, and moderate artifact reproducibility. The paper and artifact identify the target as PIM-based CNN accelerators, with ONNX CNN input, power constraint, and hardware setup parameters as inputs, and the paper states that PIMSYN generates both accelerator architecture and dataflow scheduling.

### PIMACC

- PIMACC is best understood as a narrow, artifact-level CIM accuracy simulation / verification backend tightly coupled to PIMCOMP-NN. Its real contribution is an end-to-end accuracy simulator for CIM/PIM neural-network inference under nonideal factors such as quantization error, device resistance variation, IR-drop-related effects, and stuck-at faults, using model/task/data mapping and instruction information generated by PIMCOMP-NN. Its stack style is best classified as a modeling/simulation framework and strong isolated backend component, embedded in a narrow hidden-stack flow whose inputs, mappings, schedules, and instruction descriptions are mostly inherited from PIMCOMP-NN.

### PIMCOMP

- PIMCOMP is best understood as a narrow end-to-end stack plus a strong compiler/mapping/scheduling contribution for crossbar-based PIM/CIM DNN inference. Its real contribution is a parameterized hardware template, array-group mapping abstraction, weight-replication/layout optimizer, HT/LL scheduling algorithms, pseudo-instruction stream, profiler, and released PIMCOMP-NN implementation that lowers ONNX-style DNNs into simulator-consumable instruction/config artifacts. The paper evidences an end-to-end flow from ONNX DNN models to pseudo-instructions and performance metrics, but the frontend, IR, backend, and reproducibility harness are not general-purpose: the artifact documents a limited operator set, static assumptions, architecture-specific configuration files, and some manual/hard-coded restrictions. The closest classification is Compiler/mapping framework with narrow full-stack integration, not a reusable full-stack CIM infrastructure with formal IR boundaries.

## Jianlei Yang’s Computational Intelligence Laboratory, CI-Lab, at Beihang University

### CIMFLOW

- CIMFlow’s real infrastructure contribution is a narrow but coherent vertical flow for SRAM-based digital CIM DNN inference: ONNX model input plus hardware config, CG-level graph partitioning and multi-core mapping, OP-level MLIR/DSL-based lowering, a documented 32-bit CIM ISA, and a SystemC cycle-level simulator that reports latency, energy, throughput, and utilization. It is best understood as a narrow end-to-end stack with meaningful compiler/mapping framework, ISA, and modeling/simulation framework components. Its strongest reusable pieces are the hardware hierarchy, ISA, per-core instruction JSON, synchronization model, and simulator interface; its weakest infrastructure evidence is the lack of an independently specified, provenance-rich, verifier-backed IR and the narrow convolution/MVM-centered workload/compiler assumptions.

### MIREDO

- MIREDO is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework with a stall-aware analytical modeling component. Its real contribution is a Mixed-Integer Programming formulation for CIM dataflow optimization, including a hierarchical SRAM-CIM accelerator abstraction, factorized loop-nest mapping variables, operand-specific memory-level mapping, single/double-buffer decisions, transfer-path constraints, and a recursive latency model for pipeline stalls. The paper claims ONNX input and a “complete dataflow” output, but the evidenced strength is the mapper/scheduler/cost-model layer. It fits mainly as a layer-specific strong contribution and compiler/mapping framework, with some traits of a narrow hidden-stack flow because the paper shows a path from ONNX/model description to simulator-backed evaluation without exposing the intermediate representation or plugin boundaries.

### CIMinus

- CIMinus is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework with a strong sparsity-abstraction and mapping-description contribution. Its real stack contribution is the FlexBlock sparsity abstraction plus a declarative workload / hardware / mapping interface for exploring sparse DNNs on digital SRAM-based CIM systems, with latency and energy estimated by a system-level/cycle-level cost model. The paper gives meaningful evidence for sparse-DNN cost modeling, mapping exploration, pruning-mask generation, and validation against reported MARS and SDP results. The best classification is Modeling/simulation framework + strong isolated abstraction layer, with a narrow integrated workflow from pruning to system-level evaluation. The paper itself frames CIMinus as a cost-modeling framework, explicitly says it is not a synthesis tool or accelerator implementation, and describes a decoupled interface for DNN workload DAGs, hardware units, and mapping templates.

## Purdue / Kaushik Roy / NRL

### PUMA

- PUMA’s real contribution is a hardware-software co-design demo and narrow end-to-end stack for a specific ISA-programmable hybrid CMOS-memristor inference accelerator: it defines a PUMA microarchitecture, a PUMA-specific ISA, a compiler that lowers high-level model descriptions into per-core/per-tile PUMA assembly, and a detailed architectural simulator, PUMAsim. The paper is strongest as a layer-specific contribution around ISA, mapping/scheduling, and backend simulation for one architecture, not as an auditable, backend-agnostic CIM IR or reusable plugin-style community stack. Its “programmability” claim is supported for static, MVM-heavy ML inference workloads such as MLPs, LSTMs, and CNNs, but the paper’s abstraction boundaries are mostly internal to the compiler/simulator rather than exposed as a portable IR with verifiers, provenance, conformance tests, or replaceable plugins. 

### GENIEx

- GENIEx is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework and strong isolated backend/nonideality layer. Its real contribution is a learned surrogate model for data-dependent analog memristive-crossbar MVM nonidealities, trained from HSPICE-generated voltage/conductance/current data, plus a PyTorch functional simulator that lowers selected DNN layers into tiled, bit-sliced MVM execution for accuracy-impact studies. The “end-to-end” aspect is narrow: PyTorch DNN layer simulation → MVM tiling/bit slicing → GENIEx nonideality injection → classification accuracy, with no explicit IR, no compiler pass interface, no ISA, no runtime, no memory/communication timing, no energy/area model, and no hardware chip validation. The paper’s strongest stack contribution is a calibrated nonideality plugin concept for analog CIM MVMs; its weakest aspects for our project are auditability, layer replaceability, and reproducibility. The paper itself claims an end-to-end framework and potential universal modeling, but the evidenced artifact is a fixed functional simulator for Conv/Linear layers and ResNet image-classification experiments, with explicit statement that the simulator ignores memory and communication. 

### NAX

- NAX is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework plus hardware-software co-design demo. Its real contribution is a differentiable NAS-style search procedure that jointly selects CNN layer choices, especially convolution kernel choices, and per-layer memristive crossbar sizes under accuracy, energy, and area-normalized latency objectives for a PUMA-like analog memristive crossbar accelerator. The strongest infrastructure idea is the search-space coupling between neural operations and CIM hardware binding, with nonideality-aware feedback through GENIEx and hardware-efficiency feedback through a per-operation lookup table. It does not provide an auditable IR, frontend-agnostic ingestion, ISA abstraction, plugin interface, public NAX artifact, or general backend contract; therefore it is a narrow co-design/search flow with useful IR lessons but limited direct stack reuse. The paper’s own contributions are framed as analyzing crossbar-size dependence, building a NAS framework over DNN and hardware design parameters, and demonstrating energy/latency/accuracy co-optimization.

### HASTILY

- HASTILY is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo / narrow end-to-end stack for BERT-style transformer encoder inference on an SRAM-based analog CIM accelerator. Its real contribution is a CIM microarchitecture plus scheduling/softmax acceleration scheme: dual-function 8T-SRAM “Unified Compute and Lookup Modules” that support both MVM and exponent lookup, multi-core softmax reductions, a fine-grained vector pipeline for transformer encoder layers, and a BERT-tailored compiler/ISA extension evaluated through a PUMASim-derived cycle-level simulator. The paper provides substantial architectural and scheduling evidence, but it does not evidence an auditable IR, documented frontend contract, plugin-style compiler stack, backend-agnostic interface, released artifact, or reproducibility harness.

## MIT Sze/Emer

### CIMLoop

- CiMLoop’s real contribution is a modeling/simulation framework with a strong hardware-aware specification and cost-model layer. It extends Timeloop+Accelergy with CiM-specific YAML specifications, container hierarchies, component reuse directives, data-value-dependent energy modeling, encoding/slicing support, and fast statistical amortization over mappings. Its best classification is Modeling/simulation framework plus compiler/mapping framework support, because it relies on Timeloop for spatial and temporal mapping search and Accelergy-style component models, but it does not provide a general workload frontend, formal auditable IR, ISA, runtime, hardware API, or chip-in-loop path. For our CIM infrastructure project, CiMLoop is most valuable as an IR inspiration and simulator/backend plugin candidate, especially for representing component hierarchies, tensor-specific reuse, encoding/slicing, operand-value distributions, and calibrated energy models.

### Turbo-Charged Mapper

- This paper is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework contribution. Its real contribution is a mapping-search abstraction and algorithm: it separates dataplacement, dataflow, and tile shapes in LoopTree-style mappings, then uses optimality-preserving pruning and a curried analytical model to find optimal mappings for static tensor-algebra accelerator workloads within the defined mapspace. The strongest stack layer is the mapping/scheduling abstraction plus analytical cost-model acceleration; the weak or absent layers are standard ML frontends, CIM-specific IR, ISA, runtime, chip-in-loop interface, analog nonideality modeling, and full reproducibility of paper figures. It is most accurately classified as a layer-specific strong contribution and compiler/mapping framework, with some modeling/simulation-framework integration through LoopTree, HWComponents, Timeloop, CiMLoop, and the broader AccelForge ecosystem.

##  Energy Efficient Computing group in the Nanoscale Integrated Circuits and System Lab at Tsinghua University

### Hybrid PIM for attention-free LLMs

- This paper’s real contribution is a hardware-software co-design demo for attention-free LLM inference, centered on two operator-specific PIM techniques: PN, a PIM-oriented exponent-free non-uniform weight format for RRAM analog MVMs, and multiplication-free approximate FP16 EWM execution on a proposed 3D-SRAM digital PIM array. The strongest stack contribution is a precision and arithmetic abstraction plus architecture mapping for the MVM/EWM pattern in Mamba and RWKV, backed by equations, Algorithm 1, hardware diagrams, accuracy tables, and hybrid evaluation using MNSIM 2.0, Synopsys Design Compiler at TSMC 28 nm, and CACTI. Best classification: hardware-software co-design demo, with a strong isolated precision/mapping layer, and a narrow end-to-end evaluation over Mamba/RWKV rather than a general compiler or reproducible stack.

### UniNDP

- UniNDP’s real contribution is a near-DRAM processing compilation/simulation infrastructure: it contributes a configurable tree-based DRAM-NDP hardware abstraction, a small NDP instruction abstraction, a partition/mapping search compiler, a performance predictor, and an instruction-driven DRAM-timing simulator for ML operators. The paper is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework plus compiler/mapping framework for DRAM-based NDP/PIM architectures, with a narrow end-to-end path from operator descriptions to instruction lists and latency metrics. The strongest evidence is in the hardware abstraction, instruction set, simulator, and mapping/search equations, while frontend ingestion, semantic preservation, scheduling modularity, and reproducibility are narrower and more artifact-specific. The paper itself says UniNDP focuses mainly on NN/LLM workloads, takes operator-level IR and hardware configuration as inputs, and then searches partition/mapping/scheduling strategies before simulating top-K instruction sequences.

### DyPIM

- DyPIM is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo for dynamic DNN inference on an RRAM/PIM accelerator. Its real contribution is the combination of a PIM-aware dynamic-pruning network design called Channel-Net, a channel grouping plus dynamic operation-unit formation strategy, a throughput-oriented training loss, and a modified MNSIM 2.0 behavior-level evaluation for ResNet-style CNN inference. The paper provides useful abstractions around mask readiness, spatial/channel masks, operation-unit granularity, and bottleneck-layer latency. The strongest stack layers are the dynamic-inference hardware model, mask/dataflow control, and latency-aware training objective; the weakest layers are frontend generality, IR auditability, reproducibility, backend replaceability, and fair cross-platform comparison. The appropriate classification is narrow hardware-software co-design demo / hidden-stack full-stack, with good IR inspiration for dynamic-mask CIM execution. DyPIM’s abstract and contributions explicitly frame the work as software-hardware co-optimization for dynamic inference on PIM, with reported 1.52×–2.74× speedup and 2.05×–3.95× throughput improvement over baseline ResNet networks.

### Gibbon

- Gibbon’s real contribution is not an auditable CIM IR; it is best understood as a hardware-software co-design / NAS-style co-exploration framework for memristor/RRAM PIM accelerators, combining a constrained PIM-oriented neural-network/hardware search space, the ESAPP search strategy, and an RNN-based surrogate predictor backed by MNSIM 2.0. Its strongest contribution is the co-exploration loop and cost-model surrogate, while the frontend, IR, mapper, scheduler, ISA, backend interface, and reproducibility harness are either implicit, inherited, or narrow. Classification: narrow end-to-end stack + modeling/simulation framework + hardware-software co-design demo, with useful IR lessons around design-choice variables and metric provenance.

## Tsinghua LEMON / School of Integrated Circuits leading by Huaqiang Wu

### OpenCIMTC

- OpenCIMTC is best understood as a narrow end-to-end hardware-software co-design stack for a specific memristor/RRAM CIM prototype, with a useful YAML CIM-IR, ONNX-based compiler flow, simulator/hardware driver backend, post-training/deployment training support, and simulator-/chip-in-the-loop hardware-parameter optimization. Its real infrastructure contribution is a concrete vertical flow that exposes a valuable three-way split between algorithm attributes, mapping information, and hardware calculation parameters. The paper’s strongest evidenced layers are hardware-backed CIM execution, mapping-to-array metadata, tunable deployment parameters, and on-chip evaluation; its weakest evidenced layers are frontend generality, formal IR auditability, plugin modularity, ISA stability, and independent reproducibility beyond a simulator demo.

### CLEAR

- CLEAR is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo with a narrow end-to-end stack and a strong chip-in-loop backend idea. Its real contribution is the coupling of chip-aware DNN training, a compiler that performs operator fusion/splitting plus critical-path resource reallocation, an “emulation-oriented IR” that carries real-chip or virtual backend addresses, and a calibrated analog RRAM CIM emulator that can switch between simulated execution and real-chip execution. The strongest stack layers are the RRAM CIM backend model, chip-in-loop calibration path, and mapping/address abstraction; the weakest are reusable frontend support, formal IR auditability, modular compiler/plugin boundaries, artifact reproducibility, and backend-agnosticity. The paper’s own claim is “full-stack,” but the evidenced scope is DNN-centric, ONNX-oriented, analog RRAM-specific, and evaluated on a small set of CNN/ResNet-style workloads and prior RRAM chips.

### CIMA_COMP

- CIMA_COMP is best understood as a narrow, CIMA-specific compiler/mapping/code-generation artifact. Its real stack contribution is a concrete pipeline that can import an ONNX-style workload, represent it in a YAML/JSON IR-like graph, map it onto a fixed CIMA topology, attach CIMA mapping/calculation parameters, and emit SystemC/UVM/chip-side JSON/register/weight/LUT artifacts for a YOLOv5m-style demo. The best classification is Narrow end-to-end stack plus Hidden-stack full-stack plus Strong isolated CIMA mapper/backend layer: it has useful IR/mapping/backend abstractions, but they are tied to one architecture, several hidden constants, external training artifacts, fixed data layouts, and CIMA-specific naming conventions.

### RESCIM

- ReSCIM is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo and strong isolated hardware/modeling contribution. Its real contribution is a hybrid MLC ReRAM + SRAM CIM cell/macro/accelerator architecture: compact ReRAM storage is integrated near/inside SRAM-CIM cells, differential sensing loads MLC ReRAM weights into SRAM locally, and a folded weight mapping strategy improves robustness to ReRAM/process variation. The evidenced stack layers are device/circuit/macro architecture, weight-loading/mapping, system-level simulation, and hardware/accuracy metric reporting for DNN inference.

## UVA LAVAlab / Skadron–Venkat

### PIMeval

- PIMeval / PIMbench is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework plus benchmark/evaluation framework for digital DRAM-PIM, with a high-level C++ PIM API that acts as an implicit command abstraction. Its real contribution to a CIM/PIM infrastructure stack is a reusable benchmark suite, configurable DRAM-PIM performance/energy model, and manually programmed PIM API layer covering three digital DRAM-PIM targets: DRAM-AP-style subarray bit-serial, Fulcrum-style subarray bit-parallel, and bank-level bit-parallel PIM.

### HARMONI

- HARMONI’s real contribution is a modeling/simulation framework for LLM inference on hierarchical near-/in-memory DRAM-PIM style systems. Its strongest evidenced pieces are: a Python-generated task graph that the authors describe as an intermediate representation/execution trace, custom tensor memory allocation with address/memory-location metadata, mapping of task-graph nodes to hierarchical logic units, and analytic simulation of compute/communication latency, energy, area, and critical path. It is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework plus a narrow end-to-end stack for Transformer/LLM inference over a particular class of DRAM-PIM / chiplet memory-system configurations. For our project, HARMONI is most useful as a simulator backend + benchmark harness inspiration, with useful IR lessons around tensor placement, phase-aware LLM task graphs, hierarchical memory binding, and communication edges.

### PIMsynth

- PIMsynth is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework with a narrow end-to-end stack for fixed-width bit-serial DRAM PIM kernels. Its real contribution is an automated path from Verilog bit-parallel combinational logic + GenLib-defined bit-serial PIM ISA + register count to scheduled/register-allocated bit-serial microprograms and PIMeval-based functional/performance validation. The strong layer is the bit-level lowering path for digital bit-serial PIM and analog TRA/DCC-style bit-serial PIM, including analog-specific transformations for input-destructive majority operations. The paper’s “unified” and “end-to-end” claims are evidenced within this bit-serial logic-to-PIM-microprogram scope, but the scope is explicitly bounded by two programming models and leaves broader PIM architectures and high-level language support to future work.

## Shimeng Yu’s Laboratory for Emerging Devices and Circuits

### NeuroSIM

- NeuroSim/DNN+NeuroSim is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework with a narrow end-to-end DNN-to-CIM evaluation flow. Its real contribution is the integration of a PyTorch/TensorFlow or PyTorch-only DNN wrapper with the NeuroSim C++ circuit macro model so that quantized CNN inference or training traces can be mapped onto a fixed chip/tile/PE/synaptic-array hierarchy and evaluated for accuracy, area, latency, energy, leakage, throughput, and energy efficiency. The strongest stack layer is the device/circuit/backend modeling layer, including SRAM/eNVM/FeFET options, ADC precision, retention or update nonidealities, and later calibration against a 40 nm RRAM CIM macro; the weakest layers are explicit IR, modular compiler interfaces, ISA/runtime abstraction, and frontend generality. The “end-to-end” claim is evidenced for a bounded workflow centered on DNN image models such as VGG and ResNet, with hand/config-file-visible hardware parameters and traces.

### NS-Cache

- NS-Cache — formally “Optimization and Benchmarking of Monolithically Stackable Gain Cell Memory for Last-Level Cache” — is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework and hardware-software co-design demo for advanced last-level cache memories. Its real infrastructure contribution is a cache PPA exploration tool, derived from NeuroSim/DESTINY/NVSim-style modeling, extended to advanced FinFET/nanosheet nodes and monolithic 3D AOS gain-cell memories, plus a modified Gem5/Ruby path for timing-aware cache refresh/access evaluation. The strong layer is the backend memory/device/circuit/cache model plus architectural benchmarking bridge; the weak or absent layers are high-level workload frontends, tensor/CIM IR, modular compiler passes, CIM scheduling, ISA, and auditable intermediate artifacts. The paper claims an open-source early-exploration framework for advanced cache designs and Gem5 benchmarking, and that claim is substantially evidenced for cache-memory exploration.

## SAFARI Research Group at ETH Zurich and Carnegie Mellon University

### PrIM

- PrIM is best understood as a benchmark/evaluation framework plus real-hardware characterization. Its real contribution to a CIM/PIM infrastructure stack is a concrete, open workload suite and empirical characterization methodology for the UPMEM digital DRAM-PIM architecture, including microbenchmarks, 16 manually ported workloads, CPU/GPU baselines, and hardware measurements on 640-DPU and 2,556-DPU systems. The strongest stack layer is the benchmark/reproducibility layer tied to a real hardware backend; the weakest layers are frontend ingestion, IR explicitness, compiler modularity, backend replaceability, and auditability of intermediate lowering decisions. It should be classified primarily as a benchmark/evaluation framework and strong backend / weak frontend artifact, with useful lessons for our project’s reproducibility harness and hardware-aware IR constraints.

### SparseP

- SparseP is best understood as a layer-specific strong contribution plus a narrow hardware-software co-design demo for sparse linear algebra on real near-bank digital PIM. Its real contribution is a family of 25 hand-written SpMV kernels for UPMEM DPUs, covering CSR/COO/BCSR/BCOO formats, multiple data types, 1D/2D partitioning, PIM-core and tasklet load balancing, and real-system evaluation against CPU/GPU baselines. For a CIM infrastructure project, SparseP is valuable mainly as a stress case for sparse mapping, host-PIM data movement, PIM-local scheduling, phase-level metrics, and reproducibility pitfalls.

### SimplePIM

- SimplePIM is best understood as a layer-specific programming/runtime framework plus a narrow UPMEM end-to-end flow. Its real contribution is a C-level array programming abstraction for real UPMEM-style PIM systems: a host-side management interface, host–PIM and PIM–PIM communication primitives, and processing iterators such as map, reduce, and zip, evaluated on six UPMEM workloads. It is most accurately classified as Layer-specific strong contribution, Narrow end-to-end stack, Strong backend / weak frontend, and Reproducible artifact with narrow scope: strong for programming real UPMEM PIM arrays, weak for auditable cross-layer integration. The paper’s own evidence is real-hardware UPMEM execution on six array/ML-style kernels, code release, LoC reduction, and runtime comparison against hand-optimized UPMEM baselines.

### DaPPA

- DaPPA is best understood as a layer-specific programming/compiler framework for UPMEM-style processing-in-memory. Its real contribution is a C++ data-parallel pattern frontend plus a dynamic template-based code generator that lowers map, reduce, filter, window, and group pipelines into UPMEM DPU code while managing host↔DPU data movement, MRAM/WRAM placement, alignment, CPU post-processing, and DPU/tasklet partitioning. The strongest stack layer is the UPMEM-specific frontend-to-backend programming abstraction; the weakest layers for your project are explicit IR, backend-agnostic lowering, auditable serialization, simulator interfaces, hardware-model modularity, and reproducibility artifact support. The paper’s evidence is a real UPMEM evaluation on six PrIM workloads. I classify it primarily as a compiler/mapping framework and narrow end-to-end stack for programmable near-memory PIM.

### PIM-TC

- PIM-TC is best understood as a narrow end-to-end hardware-software co-design demo and graph benchmark candidate for a real commercial PIM platform. Its real contribution is a hand-engineered triangle-counting implementation for UPMEM PIM DPUs, with a workload-specific frontend for COO graphs, a vertex-coloring partitioner, host-to-DPU batch creation, optional uniform/reservoir sampling, Misra-Gries high-degree-node handling, and measurements on real UPMEM hardware. The strongest stack layer is the real-hardware backend plus PIM-aware graph mapping strategy, while the weakest are frontend generality, IR auditability, compiler modularity, ISA abstraction, and reusable reproducibility harness.

### PIM-Opt

- PIM-Opt is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo and reproducible benchmark/evaluation framework for distributed ML training on a real UPMEM PIM system. Its real contribution is the implementation and measurement of three centralized distributed optimization algorithms—MA-SGD, GA-SGD, and ADMM—for LR/SVM training on two large datasets using real UPMEM hardware, plus CPU/GPU baselines and an artifact. The strong layer is the real-hardware backend plus workload/algorithm benchmarking, especially exposing host-DPU communication and synchronization bottlenecks. The weak layers for an infrastructure-stack project are frontend generality, IR auditability, pass modularity, backend replaceability, and ISA-level abstraction. I classify it mainly as hardware-software co-design demo + benchmark/evaluation framework, with a narrow end-to-end stack around UPMEM and linear-model training.

## Fengbin Tu HKUST

### Exploiting the Memory-Compute-Coupling Feature for CIM Accelerator Design Optimization

- This paper’s real contribution appears to be an architecture-mapping design-space exploration framework for SRAM CIM accelerators, called MCC-DSE, centered on the “memory-compute-coupling” problem: choosing the macro memory-compute ratio and mapping layers so that CIM memory capacity, CIM compute parallelism, layer fusion, layer pinning, and memory traffic are co-optimized. The strongest explicit abstraction is the three-axis CIM division mapping, which interleaves multiple layers inside each macro and reserves part of CIM memory for layer pinning; the strongest evidenced result is a reported 1.4×–8.3× EDP reduction versus a baseline architecture/mapping across workloads and chip areas. This is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework and layer-specific strong contribution.

### PIM-HLS

- PIM-HLS is best understood as a narrow end-to-end stack plus compiler/mapping framework for heterogeneous SRAM/RRAM PIM CNN accelerators. Its real contribution is a hardware-aware optimization flow that takes a manually described NN graph, uses MNSIM-derived layer latency/area choices, selects SRAM- or RRAM-based PIM per layer, schedules/remaps weights under area constraints, optimizes tile-level hardware parameters, and emits Verilog-style HDL plus claimed module instructions. The strongest evidenced layer is the mapping/scheduling/design-space optimization layer; the frontend, IR, ISA, runtime, backend interface, and reproducibility story are much less general and are either custom, implicit, or only partially exposed.

### AutoDCIM

- AutoDCIM is best understood as a layer-specific hardware EDA/compiler contribution for digital CIM macro generation. Its real contribution is a spec-to-layout digital CIM macro compiler: public evidence says it takes user specifications and generates a DCIM macro architecture with optimized layout using template-based generation and an array-partitioning/layout exploration loop. It is not evidenced as a workload frontend, graph IR, CIM system compiler, scheduler, ISA, runtime, simulator framework, or benchmark harness. Stack style: strong isolated layer + narrow hardware compiler/macro-generation flow, with some hidden-stack/full-stack language around automation but weak public evidence for auditability, modularity, or reproducibility.

### AdaP-CIM

- AdaP-CIM is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo and strong isolated hardware/numeric-format contribution. Its real contribution is an Adaptive Posit number format, AdaP, with a bounded regime length and exponent-extension mode, plus a speculative alignment unit for finding the maximum exponent before CIM MAC execution; the paper evaluates this through BERT/GLUE quantization accuracy and synthesized area/power of a 256×64 SRAM-CIM macro in TSMC 28 nm.

### SynDCIM

- SynDCIM is best understood as a layer-specific, performance-aware DCIM macro compiler and hardware-software co-design demo. Its real contribution is a macro-specification-to-layout EDA flow for SRAM-based digital CIM macros: user supplies architectural and PPA specifications, SynDCIM searches a subcircuit library, emits RTL/netlists, drives commercial synthesis/APR, and validates one generated macro in 40 nm silicon. The paper’s “end-to-end” claim is valid only within the DCIM macro generation stack, from macro-level specs to layout and chip measurement; it does not evidence a PyTorch/ONNX/MLIR/TVM frontend, high-level tensor IR, workload mapper, ISA, runtime, benchmark harness, or reusable backend interface. The strongest stack layers are subcircuit library, PPA-aware architectural search, RTL/netlist generation, APR flow, and silicon validation. The weakest layers for our project are auditable IR, frontend ingestion, mapping/scheduling across workloads, reproducibility artifact, and plugin replaceability. The paper was published at DATE 2025, and the public project page I found links only to the paper, not to a code artifact.

## Tianyu Jia’s PACIFIC Lab at PKU

### ARES

- ARES is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework and hardware-abstraction proposal for mapping static DNN tensor operators onto several classes of PIM/CIM-like hardware. Its real contribution is a pair of abstractions: a compute abstraction that represents a PIM primitive as a tensorized compute equation, and a memory abstraction that represents bit-level operand layout constraints with mapping matrices and offsets; these are then used to build and search a mapping space covering binding, tiling, resource allocation, execution order, and data transfer. It has useful IR-like ideas, especially for encoding PIM-specific operand layout and legality constraints. The best classification is layer-specific strong contribution / compiler-mapping framework, with some modeling/simulation support through custom cycle-accurate simulators.

### Reconfigurable-dataflow CIM accelerator for multi-scale Vision Transformer

- This paper’s real contribution is a digital SRAM-CIM accelerator architecture and dataflow for static-shape, image-domain multi-scale Vision Transformers with hybrid convolution + MHA topology. It is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo / layer-specific architecture contribution with a narrow vertical evaluation path: the evidenced strengths are MHA pipeline reordering, a two-stage softmax placement across IMC macros and distributor nodes, fused MHA matrix multiplication to reduce quadratic intermediate traffic, reconfigurable IMC engines/interconnects, and a memory hierarchy around DMA, unified SRAM banks, reuse buffers, accumulation, requantization, and activation. The paper reports TSMC 22 nm evaluation with 2.20×–2.52× speedup and 40.6%–74.8% energy savings for MHA workloads, plus 44.1%–55.9% EDP reduction for typical multi-scale ViTs versus a baseline IMC accelerator, but I found no evidenced public frontend, serializable IR, verifier, compiler API, ISA, simulator API, or reproducibility harness.

### CIM-MXU

- CIM-MXU is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo plus modeling/simulation framework. Its real contribution is a digital SRAM CIM replacement for TPU-style matrix multiply units, embedded into a TPUv4i-like architecture model and evaluated on representative generative-model inference kernels, especially GPT-3-style LLM layers and DiT blocks. The paper’s strongest stack layer is the CIM-MXU microarchitecture/model and TPU-level design-space exploration: it defines a CIM-core grid, systolic propagation, BF16/INT8 support, memory hierarchy assumptions, and evaluates latency/energy/area trade-offs. The stack style is therefore: narrow end-to-end stack, strong isolated hardware/modeling layer, and hidden-stack full-stack.

## Southeast University CIM macro/circuit group

### CIM-Tuner: hardware-mapping co-exploration framework

- CIM-Tuner is best understood as a compiler/mapping + modeling/simulation framework for SRAM-CIM hardware/mapping co-exploration. Its real contribution is a matrix-template abstraction for SRAM-CIM macros, a generalized accelerator template, a two-level mapping space, and a simulated-annealing/heuristic exploration flow that chooses accelerator sizing and per-operator mappings under area/objective constraints. The paper claims an automatic, universal, open-sourced tool and provides meaningful evidence for mapping/hardware co-exploration, instruction-flow simulation, and limited silicon-backed model validation; however, its frontend is largely manual operator-shape CSV input, its “IR” is not an independently documented auditable IR, and its backend is tightly coupled to its own compiler/simulator scripts. The best classification is: narrow end-to-end stack + compiler/mapping framework + modeling/simulation framework + hardware-software co-design demo. The DATE/arXiv paper states the core claims as automatic hardware balancing and optimal mapping under area constraints, using a matrix abstraction, generalized accelerator template, two-level mapping strategies, and reporting 1.58× energy-efficiency and 2.11× throughput improvement over a previous CIM mapping baseline.

### AccelCIM

- AccelCIM is best understood as a modeling/simulation framework plus SRAM-CIM macro-array dataflow exploration framework. Its real contribution is a structured design space for SRAM CIM macro parameters and macro-array organizations, especially weight-stationary vs. output-stationary, broadcast vs. systolic interconnect, and compute-I/O overlap vs. no overlap, coupled to a hybrid evaluation flow using an in-house cycle simulator, SPICE-derived macro characterization, RTL generation, Cadence synthesis/place-and-route, and post-layout PPA extraction. It is strongest as a hardware-aware mapping/scheduling and backend PPA evaluation study for GEMM-like LLM inference on digital SRAM CIM; it is weak as a frontend, IR, ISA, runtime, or reproducible community stack because the paper does not expose a documented IR, external simulator interface, frontend import path, artifact, tests, scripts, or replayable intermediate states.

## Fudan Chixiao Chen CiH Lab

### ARCTIC

- ARCTIC is best understood as a layer-specific physical DCIM macro compiler / hardware-generation flow. Its real contribution is a parameterized SRAM-based digital CIM macro generator that accepts macro-level hardware specifications such as timing and area constraints, memory depth and width, mantissa width, exponent width, and exponent offset, then generates low-level RTL, BIST logic, and post-layout macro implementations through commercial EDA flows. The strongest contribution is the combination of INT/FP precision-parameterized macro generation and MarchCIM BIST support; the weakest part, for a reusable CIM infrastructure stack, is that there is no explicit auditable IR, no model frontend, no tensor-to-array mapping from real DNN graphs, no instruction abstraction, and no released artifact. I classify it primarily as a strong isolated layer and narrow end-to-end macro-generation stack, with secondary value as IR inspiration for precision, macro layout, and CIM-specific DFT/BIST constructs.

### CIM-Pruner: Dual-Mode CIM Macro for Efficient VLMs

- CIM-Pruner’s evidenced contribution is not a full CIM infrastructure stack, but a hardware/circuit-level CIM macro design aimed at VLM acceleration through in-memory token pruning and token merging. Based on public evidence, it is best understood as a hardware-software co-design demo / strong isolated hardware layer, with possible algorithm-hardware coupling around intra-chunk token reduction. The important infrastructure lesson is that VLM-oriented CIM backends may require first-class representation of dynamic token masks, merge groups, chunk boundaries, mode switches, and token lineage, but the currently available evidence does not show a reusable frontend, IR, mapping API, backend interface, or artifact.

## Others

### Polyhedral-Based Compilation Framework for In-MemoryNeural Network Accelerators

- Polyhedral is best understood as a compiler/mapping framework with a narrow source-to-source vertical flow. Its real contribution is using the polyhedral model to detect affine NN loop kernels, identify MV/MM/CONV/pooling and fused init/activation patterns, transform them into calls or marked schedule regions for memristor-based NN accelerators, and optionally generate a pipeline/resource allocation across matched operators. The paper claims automatic detection/mapping of multiple NN operators and pipeline generation for memristor-based architectures, with evaluation on synthetic kernels and NN benchmarks and case studies on typical architectures. The artifact confirms a C/PET/ISL source-to-source compiler with tiling, PE-count, pipeline, and coarse/conv options.

### SEGA-DCIM

- SEGA-DCIM is best understood as a hardware-generation and design-space-exploration framework for digital CIM macros, not as a workload-to-CIM compiler stack. Its real contribution is a template-based DCIM macro generator plus an NSGA-II / MOGA design-space explorer over macro parameters, precision, and architecture template choice, producing Pareto-frontier DCIM designs and layouts through commercial digital EDA tools. The strongest evidenced layers are macro architecture parameterization, analytical PPA estimation, multi-precision INT/FP design exploration, and template-based Verilog/netlist/layout generation. The paper does not evidence a PyTorch/ONNX/MLIR frontend, graph/tensor IR, CIM instruction abstraction, runtime, full-system simulator interface, or reproducible public artifact. I classify it primarily as a modeling / hardware-generation framework and secondarily as a narrow end-to-end hardware macro flow, where “compiler” means “DCIM circuit/layout generator,” not a reusable software compiler from workloads to CIM execution. The paper was submitted to arXiv in May 2025 and is listed by an author publication page as a DATE 2025 paper.

### OpenACMv2

- OpenACMv2 is best understood as a modeling/simulation and architecture–circuit co-optimization framework for approximate digital SRAM-based CIM/DCiM. Its real contribution is an accuracy-constrained two-level ACCO flow: Level I searches approximate multiplier compressor assignments, partial-product approximation patterns, and SRAM macro parameters using a PEA-GNN surrogate and multi-objective optimizers; Level II performs transistor-level sizing for compressors and SRAM bitcells with SPICE/PVT/Monte Carlo-style evaluation. The strongest stack layer is the hardware-aware design-space exploration/cost-model layer for 8/16-bit approximate multipliers and SRAM macros; the weakest layers are workload ingestion, high-level IR, general mapping/scheduling, ISA/runtime, and benchmark reproducibility. Overall classification: modeling/simulation framework + hardware-software co-design demo + narrow full-stack flow for a DCiM PE, with useful IR inspiration for representing approximate arithmetic choices and circuit-level validity domains.

### OpenC2

- The paper’s real contribution is not a CIM workload frontend, CIM IR, neural-network compiler, instruction abstraction, runtime, or benchmark harness; it is a template-based hardware macro generator / physical-design flow for digital SRAM-CIM macros, taking top-level macro parameters and technology/cell-library inputs and producing Verilog/SPICE plus DEF/GDSII layout artifacts. The “end-to-end” claim is credible only inside the DCIM macro hardware-generation slice, from macro-level parameters and library files to netlist/layout; it does not evidence an application-to-hardware CIM stack. Best classification: strong isolated hardware-generation backend with a narrow end-to-end macro flow, plus hidden-stack full-stack behavior because many mapping, timing, technology, and layout assumptions are embedded in templates, scripts, and generated EDA files rather than in an auditable CIM IR. The paper and artifact are useful to our project mainly as a backend-plugin case study and as a warning that “open-source end-to-end” can still mean a parameterized hard-coded macro generator rather than an inspectable, modular full-stack CIM infrastructure. The paper states that OpenC² generates front-end netlists in Verilog/SPICE and back-end layouts in DEF/GDSII from top-level specifications, using reference 45 nm FreePDK cells and open-source tools; the public repository exists, but its README is minimal and the license/runnable workflow are not clearly evidenced.
